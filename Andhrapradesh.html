Ancient Civilization

The ancient civilization of Andhra Pradesh has a rich history that dates back to the Vedic period. It is mentioned in Sanskrit epics such as the Aitareya Brahmana (800 BCE). The region was known as Assaka during the sixth-century BCE and was one of the sixteen mahajanapadas (700–300 BCE). Amaravati, which was known as Dhānyakatakam, is particularly significant as it was the capital of the Satavahana Dynasty of the Andhras, one of India’s earliest empires, around the second century BCE

Kakatiya dynasty

The Kakatiya dynasty rose to power during the 12th and 13th centuries. Initially vassals of the Western Chalukyas of Kalyani, they held a small territory near Warangal. Prola II of the Kakatiyas (1110–1158) extended his territory southwards and declared his independence, with Hanumakonda as the capital. His successor, Prataparudra I (1158–1195), increased the holdings eastward to the Godavari delta. Prataparudra built Warangal as a second capital, and countered invasions by the Seuna Yadavas of Devagiri.

Chalcolithic age

The Chalcolithic period is dated using pottery and is believed to be around 1750 BC or earlier. The pottery used for dating the time period was discovered from sites near the Krishna and Tungabhadra rivers such as Patapadu. A painted spouted vessel found there resembles chalcolithic-age vessels from Navdatoli and as far as Bronze Age Crete

Vijayanagara Empire

AndhraPradesh was part of the Vijayanagara Empire, Harihara established his new capital, Vijayanagar, in an easily defended position south of the Tungabhadra River. The empire reached its zenith under Krishnadevaraya in the early 16th century, and Telugu literature developed at this time. Vijayanagar monuments were built across South India, and in Lepakshi, Tirupati and Sri Kalahasti in Andhra Pradesh.

Cultural Diversity

Andhra Pradesh is known for its rich cultural diversity, which includes:
• Language: Telugu is the primary language, with several linguistic and tribal groups present.
• Arts: The classical dance form Kuchipudi and music genre Andhra Natyam showcase the region’s artistic heritage.
• Traditions: A blend of various traditions reflecting the state’s historical influences and agricultural prominence as the “Rice Bowl of India”.

Cultural Heritage

Andhra Pradesh is renowned for its rich cultural heritage, which includes:
• Monuments: Famous landmarks like the Charminar in Hyderabad and the Sri Venkateswara Temple in Tirupati.
• Arts & Crafts: Traditional crafts like Kalamkari textile art and intricate metalware.
• Music: A strong musical heritage with Carnatic legends like Shyama Sastri, Thyagaraja, and Muthuswami Dixtar hailing from the region.
• Cuisine: Delectable local cuisine known for its variety and flavors

Andhra Pradesh :The Rice Bowl of India


Andhra Pradesh, located in the southern coastal region of India, is a state with a rich history and diverse culture. It is often referred to as the “Kohinoor of India” due to its natural beauty and variety of wildlife1. It boasts the second-longest coastline in India, stretching about 974 kilometers (605 miles) along the Bay of Bengal.The state is known as the “Rice Bowl of India” because it produces a significant amount of rice or paddy in its coastal plains2. Agriculture, including rice cultivation, plays a crucial role in the state’s economy.Two major rivers, the Godavari and the Krishna, flow through Andhra Pradesh, providing water for irrigation and supporting agriculture and livelihoods.Andhra Pradesh took its present form on June 2, 2014, when the new state of Telangana was formed through bifurcation. Prior to that, it existed as Andhra State and unified Andhra Pradesh.
